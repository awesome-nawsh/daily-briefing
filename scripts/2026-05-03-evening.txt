Singapore

The biggest story from Singapore this week is PM Lawrence Wong's May Day Rally speech on Friday. He was candid about what's coming — the Iran crisis and US blockades on Iranian ports are already slowing growth and pushing inflation higher. But the headline pledge was clear: Singapore will protect every worker, even if it can't protect every job. That means 37 million dollars through NTUC this year, a new Tripartite Jobs Council to manage the AI transition, and a community fund with a 500 million dollar target over five years. Wong was visibly moved at one point, reading out a letter from a Singaporean repatriated from the Middle East — a rare public moment of vulnerability from a prime minister, and it landed well with the crowd. Separately, a Citi survey shows 88 percent of Singaporeans now expect prices to rise over the next year. The anxiety is real, even if actual inflation remains around 1.3 percent.

Global Geopolitics

Three things to watch globally tonight. First, the UAE has formally exited OPEC — making it the third member in seven years to leave the cartel. With peak oil demand approaching and OPEC's market share shrinking, analysts are framing this less as a tactical protest and more as a structural unravelling. Watch energy markets as that settles through the week. Second, in Europe, the Pentagon has confirmed it's withdrawing five thousand troops from Germany. Berlin's defense minister called it anticipated, but the message to European capitals is unmistakable — strategic self-reliance is now the plan, not the aspiration. Expect this to accelerate European defense spending discussions. Third, in Latin America, the US has confirmed the capture of Venezuelan President Nicolas Maduro — a significant development with knock-on effects for regional stability and oil markets. Watch for how neighbouring governments respond over the coming days.

Germany, South Africa and Australia

In Germany, that troop withdrawal story is dominating — Berlin is now under real pressure to move faster on defence spending. Tech and AI

A study published in Nature this week found that experienced human scientists still outperform the best AI agents on genuinely novel research tasks. It's a useful counterpoint to the everything-will-be-automated narrative — AI is fast and broad, but creative scientific reasoning remains human territory, at least for now.

Science and Discovery

New research suggests the Cascadia subduction zone and the San Andreas fault on the US West Coast can synchronise, triggering earthquakes within minutes of each other. If confirmed, it significantly changes risk modelling for Seattle, Portland and northern California. The science is still early, but the implications for those regions are substantial.
