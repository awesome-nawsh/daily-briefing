Good morning, Sean — it's Sunday the twelfth of April, 2026.

Here's one for you: sixty-five years ago this morning, Yuri Gagarin became the first human being to reach outer space. He launched at 9:07am Moscow time, completed one orbit of Earth in 108 minutes, and came home. What most people don't know is that he didn't land inside the capsule — he ejected and parachuted down separately, because the Soviets weren't confident the landing system could be trusted. He didn't know whether the hatch would even open. He found out the same way he found out everything else that day — by going anyway.

Over in Singapore, Parliament has moved the Securities and Futures Amendment Bill to first reading. This sets up the regulatory framework for the Global Listing Board — SGX's joint venture with Nasdaq. If it passes, Singapore becomes a significantly more attractive listing venue for international companies across Asia. Worth watching as it progresses. Also, the temporary ban on Taiwanese pork imports has been lifted following the containment of the African swine fever outbreak there. Expect Taiwanese pork back on shelves shortly.

Vice President JD Vance, special envoy Steve Witkoff, and Jared Kushner arrived in Pakistan on Friday to meet directly with Iran's parliamentary speaker and foreign minister. If the numbers hold, it could end sixteen years of Orbán in power — a significant shift for European politics and NATO cohesion. Results aren't expected until tomorrow morning.

On science — the James Webb Space Telescope has spotted what researchers are calling a forbidden planet. TOI-5205 b is a Jupiter-sized world orbiting a small, cool red dwarf star. The issue is that every planetary formation model says a star that small should not be able to produce a gas giant that large. Its atmosphere is producing readings that current science cannot explain. It does not just challenge existing models — it dismantles them.

A genuine strategic inflection point.
