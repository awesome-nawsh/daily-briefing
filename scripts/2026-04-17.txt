Your calendar is looking clear today — a clean run into the weekend.

Over in Singapore, a couple of stories worth noting. The Health Sciences Authority is rolling out a new Healthpoint reward scheme to attract more blood donors, after authorities flagged a potential supply crisis in as little as seven years if donation rates don't improve. It's a quiet infrastructure story but a meaningful one. Separately, a new employment survey suggests Singapore's hiring activity and wage growth will ease through 2026 as global risk sentiment cools — not a crisis, but consistent with a broader regional slowdown that the IMF also flagged in its Asia Pacific outlook this week.

On the global stage, Russia launched one of its largest drone and missile barrages of the year against Ukraine overnight, hitting civilian areas across multiple cities and killing at least sixteen people. The attack lasted several hours and drew immediate condemnation from European capitals. Meanwhile, a significant development in the Middle East: US and Iranian negotiators have agreed to a two-week ceasefire and the opening of the Strait of Hormuz — the first meaningful de-escalation since coordinated US-Israeli strikes on Iran's nuclear infrastructure triggered the closure in late February. Oil markets are already reacting. Crude has been above a hundred dollars a barrel for weeks, and a functioning Hormuz changes the supply picture considerably if the ceasefire holds.

Moving to Germany, South Africa, and Australia. In Germany, the Iran crisis is now hitting household budgets directly — gas prices for new customers have surged around 30 percent since the conflict began, reaching a three-year high, and Berlin's coalition government is facing growing pressure to revise a twenty-billion-euro healthcare savings package that critics say will hit residents too hard. In South Africa, EFF leader Julius Malema was sentenced to five years in prison yesterday for firing a rifle at a party rally — his lawyers have been granted leave to appeal, so this story has more chapters to come. And in Australia, Canberra announced the largest peacetime defence commitment in the country's history: three percent of GDP from 2033, starting with an extra fourteen billion dollars over the next four years, directly responding to US pressure on allies to lift their spending.

In tech and AI, a significant milestone this week. OpenAI unveiled GPT-5.4 with a one-million-token context window and the ability to independently execute multi-step workflows across software environments — no human in the loop. On real-world desktop task benchmarks, it scored 75 percent against a human baseline of 72. That's AI that genuinely matches professional-level knowledge work performance, and the implications for enterprise productivity are hard to overstate. Worth a conversation with the team if you haven't already had it.

In science, a paper this week may finally resolve a long-running dinosaur debate: Nanotyrannus — long dismissed as just a juvenile T. rex — appears to have been its own distinct species after all. New bone growth analysis shows it stopped developing at around five metres, rather than being on its way to full T. rex size. A small but satisfying win for the underdog.

On BHP, the company has closed the largest streaming deal in mining history — a four-point-three-billion-dollar silver agreement with Wheaton Precious Metals, unlocking value from the Antamina copper-zinc mine in Peru. From April first, BHP is supplying silver credits at just under 34 percent of Antamina's output. Smart capital recycling that monetises a by-product stream at real scale.

And for motorcycles, a genuinely novel debut: Omoway has revealed the OMO X, billed as the first mass-produced self-balancing electric motorbike. An AI-driven gyroscope system keeps it upright at low speeds, with adaptive cruise and collision braking on board. Whether that's the future of riding or just interesting engineering — you'll have to judge for yourself.
