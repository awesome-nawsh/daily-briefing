GLOBAL GEOPOLITICS

The dominant story worldwide remains the US-Israel war on Iran, now ten weeks in. There may be a significant development: Iran has reportedly offered to reopen the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for the US lifting its naval blockade. Trump, who has said sanctions relief comes only when a deal is one hundred percent complete, hasn't responded publicly yet. It's the most concrete de-escalation signal since the conflict began on February 28th, and markets are watching it closely. Oil is still above a hundred dollars a barrel, and the IMF now projects global growth at just 3.1 percent for 2026 with inflation running at 4.4 percent.

In Europe, Washington delivered another jolt: the Pentagon has formally begun pulling five thousand US troops out of Germany. Berlin's defence minister called it anticipated, but NATO allies are rattled — Spain and Italy are reportedly next on the list. Germany has responded with an ambitious military expansion plan targeting 460,000 combined active and reserve troops by the mid-2030s.

US-brokered Russia-Ukraine peace talks continue but remain without a decisive breakthrough. Global military spending hit a record 2.9 trillion dollars in 2025, with European expenditure surging 14 percent to its highest level ever. And Trump was the only major economy leader absent from a 60-nation climate conference in Colombia last week.

GERMANY, SOUTH AFRICA AND AUSTRALIA

Germany's government has halved its economic growth forecast for 2026, citing the oil price shock from the Iran war. Consumer confidence dropped sharply in May — the steepest monthly fall since 2022.

In South Africa, President Ramaphosa has set the local government elections for November 4th this year — worth noting given BHP's footprint in the country.

In Australia, police charged a 47-year-old man with the murder of five-year-old Indigenous girl Kumanjayi Little Baby, whose death last week sparked violent protests in the Northern Territory. The case has shaken the country and reignited hard national conversations about the safety of Indigenous children.

SCIENCE AND DISCOVERY

Oxford physicists achieved a genuine first last month: the demonstration of quadsqueezing — a fourth-order quantum effect that had been theorised for decades but never observed. It's a significant milestone in quantum physics and potentially a building block for next-generation computing and sensing.
