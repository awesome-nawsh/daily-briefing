Over in Singapore, two stories worth your attention. Yesterday saw a fairly significant broadband wobble across the island — Singtel, StarHub and M1 all reported outages from around half ten in the morning, with SIMBA, MyRepublic and ViewQwest also affected. Singtel pointed the finger at on-site construction works disrupting all operators at once, a reminder of how much of the city's connectivity sits on shared infrastructure underground. Second, the Circle Line saga is winding towards a happier chapter. LTA and SMRT finished the three-month tunnel strengthening works nine days ahead of schedule, and they're now in final systems testing for the Circle Line Stage 6 opening. Services still end earlier on Saturday nights and start later on Sunday mornings through mid-May to allow for testing, so if you're planning an early ride today, the CCL doesn't run until nine.

On the international front, the Strait of Hormuz took another turn yesterday. Iran's military announced the strait has returned to its previous state, meaning Tehran is reasserting strict control over tanker traffic after briefly opening it during the Lebanon ceasefire. Markets had rallied on that earlier opening, with oil down about eleven per cent, so expect some give-back as traders digest the new signals. US and Iranian delegations are reportedly set to meet tomorrow. Separately, a fascinating regional realignment is brewing in the Middle East. Egypt is rethinking its longstanding strategic balancing act and is reportedly moving closer to a new Sunni axis with Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Turkey and Pakistan, a notable shift in regional security architecture that will ripple through trade and energy policy for years to come.

On Germany, South Africa and Australia. Germany's Defence Minister Boris Pistorius was over in Australia this week visiting Rheinmetall's plant, where more than a hundred heavy weapon carriers are being built for the Bundeswehr, a tidy piece of Australian defence manufacturing export. In South Africa, Eskom has announced a nine-hour planned outage across parts of Gauteng on Wednesday for essential maintenance, and in lighter news, the Western Cape woke up to a rare light snowfall dusting mountain peaks, unusual for April. And Australia has committed to its largest peacetime defence spend ever, three per cent of GDP from 2033, starting with an extra fourteen billion dollars over the next four years.

In tech, Anthropic has given its new Claude model what the company is calling twenty hours of psychiatric evaluation before release. They're describing it as their most psychologically settled model yet, pitched at making AI interactions more human-like and less brittle. On the maker front, ESPHome 2026.4.0 dropped this month with a meaningful performance jump. ESP32 boards now default to 240 megahertz instead of 160, delivering about 34 per cent faster API operations, plus signed OTA updates. Worth a firmware bump on your home lab nodes this weekend.

In science, researchers have found sizeable lithium deposits hidden inside pyrite, the old fool's gold, within ancient shale rocks. If it scales, it's a genuinely new source of battery-grade lithium tied to existing mining infrastructure, with obvious implications for how the world thinks about critical mineral supply chains going forward.

One for BHP. China's state-owned iron ore buyer has signalled that steel mills can resume purchasing BHP cargoes, which looks like the beginning of a thaw in the dispute that has kept things tense these last few weeks. Worth watching how pricing and volumes settle over the coming days.

On bikes, Ducati has unveiled the new Monster plus, fifth generation, powered by a new 890cc V2 engine that's thirteen pounds lighter than the outgoing model. It's available in Ducati red or a rather handsome iceberg white if you're window-shopping.
