Good morning Sean, it's Monday, the eleventh of May, 2026.

Fun fact: twenty-nine years ago today, IBM's Deep Blue defeated chess world champion Garry Kasparov — the first computer ever to beat a reigning world champion in a full match. Kasparov demanded a rematch. He never got one.

Singapore first. The Mount Dukono story has moved from a search to a recovery. Rescue teams have located the bodies of the two missing Singaporean hikers near the crater rim, but continuous eruptions, flooding, and rain have halted retrieval operations. Both are confirmed dead, alongside a local guide. The seven surviving Singaporeans are heading home — a tragic end to a group that entered a restricted zone to create social media content.

On a brighter note, Singapore's CPF could soon become a major driver of local equities. A new proposal would redirect fund savings into the stock market, with Citi estimating annual inflows of six to nine billion dollars. And Singapore property continues to run hot despite global uncertainty.

In global geopolitics, the Strait of Hormuz is a flashpoint again. US forces exchanged fire with Iranian units last Friday and struck two Iranian tankers. Iran retaliated with missiles and drones against the UAE. The fragile ceasefire is now at serious risk of collapse, with energy prices spiking globally. Southeast Asian leaders at the ASEAN summit in Manila are already coordinating their response.

In Europe, cautious movement on Ukraine. Putin said the war may be coming to an end and signalled willingness to meet Zelenskyy in a third country. Russia and Ukraine began a three-day ceasefire and agreed to exchange a thousand prisoners. But within twenty-four hours, a Russian drone struck an apartment building in Kharkiv. Very cautious optimism.

Hungary is resetting. New Prime Minister Peter Magyar was sworn in after defeating Viktor Orban in April's elections, pledging a sharp pivot away from Moscow and back toward the EU.

And on India-Pakistan: one year since their ceasefire. The guns are quiet but diplomacy is frozen. Pakistan warned it would respond with greater force to any future Indian attack.

In Germany, Defence Minister Pistorius is heading to Washington to acquire Tomahawk missiles after the Pentagon cancelled its own planned deployment on German soil. Separately, the EU-Mercosur trade deal entered provisional force on May first.

In South Africa, hackers seized Ekurhuleni municipality's IT billing system and manipulated data to reduce property rates debts — a striking example of municipal cyber vulnerability.

In Australia, the federal government has scrapped the inland rail megaproject after a forty-five billion dollar cost blowout. The Melbourne-to-Brisbane freight link is cancelled — a major political setback.

Tech and AI: for the second month running, AI is the top reason cited for job cuts globally. Cloudflare, Upwork, and Coinbase were among those restructuring in May. The Pentagon has formally signed AI agreements with eight tech giants — SpaceX, OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, Nvidia, AWS, Oracle, and Reflection — for classified networks. And Google is rolling Gemini into cars with Google built-in, making conversational in-car AI mainstream.

Science: physicists have discovered particles that break the most fundamental rule in particle physics. For decades everything was a boson or a fermion. Researchers have found quantum particles in neither category. It could open entirely new branches of physics.

BHP: the company has finalised its iron ore supply deal with China's state buyer CMRG. Notably, BHP agreed to pricing partially linked to China's domestic BTI Index — a first among the big four miners and a meaningful shift in market power dynamics.

Motorcycles: the 2026 Ducati Monster Plus arrives in fifth generation — new 890cc V-twin, a hundred and ten horsepower, lighter and sharper. Early ride impressions are excellent.

Singapore today: highs of thirty-three to thirty-five degrees, humid, afternoon thunderstorms on most days, with possible Sumatra squalls and gusty winds on a morning or two.
