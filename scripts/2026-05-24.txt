Good morning Sean, it's Sunday, the twenty-fourth of May 2026.

Here's a fact to start the day: on this exact date in 1844, Samuel Morse sent the very first telegraph message, from Washington D.C. to Baltimore. The words he chose were "What hath God wrought?" Given everything that has happened in communications since, that question seems more apt than ever.

Your calendar is clear today - a free Sunday ahead.

In Singapore, Dr Koh Poh Koon resigned as Senior Minister of State on Friday, stepping down from his Manpower and Health portfolios effective June the first. The reason he gave was family. He said publicly he has been an absent husband, father and son since entering politics in 2015, and his family needs him now in a way that is not compatible with the demands of public office. He will remain as Tampines MP. It is a rare and candid statement about the personal cost of political life.

The biggest story globally is the US-Iran peace process. Both sides were expected to announce a draft deal this weekend. The proposed framework is substantial: Iran would commit to a moratorium on nuclear enrichment, the US would lift sanctions and release frozen Iranian funds, and the Strait of Hormuz would reopen to all shipping. After weeks of escalation and an April ceasefire, this would be a major development - though nothing is signed yet.

Trump's ten percent global tariff is tied up in the courts. The US trade court ruled it legally unjustified last week, but an appeals court immediately issued a temporary stay. The economic cost to American consumers is around fifteen hundred dollars per household this year.

Japan passed a rule allowing it to sell arms to seventeen countries - a meaningful departure from its post-war pacifist stance. India-Pakistan tensions remain unresolved a year on from their four-day air war, with India still holding the Indus Waters Treaty suspended, a treaty that underpins eighty percent of Pakistan's agricultural water. In Colombia, armed group attacks are escalating ahead of presidential elections on May 31.

In Europe, after eight years of negotiations, the EU and Australia have reached a comprehensive free trade deal. NATO spending across European allies and Canada jumped nearly twenty percent to 574 billion US dollars.

South Africa received a Moody's ratings upgrade from stable to positive - welcome news. There are still concerns over a wave of xenophobic attacks targeting foreign nationals, but murder rates dropped nine-point-five percent, and the reform story is gaining traction.

Australia's federal budget changed the rules on negative gearing and capital gains tax - a politically bold housing move. The RBA raised the cash rate to four-point-three-five percent, and there is a ten billion dollar commitment to fuel security.
