On the Singapore economy, fresh trade data makes for encouraging reading. Non-oil domestic exports jumped 15.3 percent in March, while re-exports surged a remarkable 61.4 percent, extending the gains from the previous quarter. It's a signal that Singapore's role as a regional trade hub remains very much intact despite the global headwinds. On a related note, a Citi report this week flags that local consumer inflation expectations are ticking up — 88 percent of respondents now expect prices to rise over the next year, even though actual inflation remains relatively tame at around 1.2 to 1.4 percent. Worth watching if it starts to translate into wage pressures.

Two big stories on the global front. First, the UAE has officially left OPEC — the third member to depart in seven years. Combined with the broader instability from the US-Iran conflict and its knock-on effects across the Gulf, this is quietly reshaping the architecture of global energy production. Second, the EU-Mercosur trade deal began provisional application on the first of May, creating tariff-free access for EU exporters into Argentina, Brazil, Paraguay, and Uruguay. It's the largest trade agreement the EU has ever signed by population, and signals that Europe is actively expanding its economic relationships beyond the traditional transatlantic axis.

In Germany, Washington confirmed it will withdraw around 5,000 troops in the next six to twelve months, following friction between President Trump and Chancellor Merz over US strategy in the Iran conflict. It marks a concrete step in the deteriorating NATO relationship, and European leaders are already scrambling to invoke the EU's own mutual defence clause as a contingency.

In South Africa, Cape Town residents are being warned to prepare for widespread water outages from today through to the 10th of May. And separately, a break-in at a South African National Defence Force facility in Pretoria has left military-grade weapons unaccounted for — that one is getting serious attention from security analysts.

In Australia, the Reserve Bank meets Monday and Tuesday for its next monetary policy decision. Markets are watching closely for signs of a rate cut given the global slowdown picture.

On tech, Huawei is projecting its AI chip revenue will hit roughly 12 billion US dollars this year — up sixty percent from 2025 — as demand for domestic Chinese AI infrastructure accelerates. It's a useful reminder that the global AI supply chain is bifurcating in ways that will have long-term implications for the tech sector.

If you've got nodes that have been feeling sluggish, this one is worth upgrading.

Its unusual triangular molecular structure gives it exceptional stability and reactivity, opening up the possibility of far cheaper and greener industrial chemistry. The kind of materials science breakthrough that sounds modest but could have real implications for everything from batteries to catalysts.
